Graph Clustering With Missing Data: Convex Algorithms and Analysis

  • Ramya Korlakai Vinayak, Samet Oymak, Babak Hassibi.
  • Accepted to the Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS) 2014.
  • We consider the problem of finding clusters in graphs which are partially observed. We analyze two programs based on regularized nuclear norm minimization, with a goal to recover the low- rank part of the adjacency matrix. For the commonly used Stochastic Block Model, we provide explicit conditions in terms of the size and sparsity of the clusters, amount of observed data, and the regularization parameter of the convex program that guarantees successful recovery of the clusters. We also corroborate our theoretical findings with extensive simulations, and also run our algorithms on a real data set obtained from crowdsourcing an image classification task on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

Sharp Performance Bounds for Graph Clustering via Convex Optimization

  • Ramya Korlakai Vinayak, Samet Oymak, Babak Hassibi.
  • Accepted to the 38th International Conference on Acoustics, Speech, and Signal Processing (ICASSP) 2014.
  • We analyze a popular approach towards identifying clusters in graphs, that of a sparse plus low-rank decomposition of the adjacency matrix of a graph via a simple and intuitive convex program. We sharply characterize the conditions for the success and failure of this program in identifying clusters. We also present extensive simulations that corroborate our theoretical findings.

Convex Optimization Based Algorithms for Clustering Large Graphs

  • October 2012 - ongoing
  • With: Prof. Babak Hassibi, Samet Oymak
  • We cast the problem of clustering the nodes in unweighted and weighted graphs in large networks as a convex optimization problem.
  • Our goal is to develop efficient algorithms which can work with partially observed data, and provide provable guarantees on the performance of the algorithms.

Citation Centrality and Academia Salaries

  • March 2012 - May 2012
  • With: Prof. Adam Wiermann, Kijun Seo, Michael Hirshleifer
  • We investigated whether measures of productivity based on citation centrality could explain academic salary.
  • We analyzed 18 years of citation network data for Theoretical High Energy Physics to compute various centrality measures. We obtained the salary data of professors in public schools, and fitted a linear model incorporating the measure of centrality and seniority.
  • Our study showed that the simple measure of citation count and seniority predicted salary with high confidence while sophisticated measures (like h- and g-indicies) failed to do so.

Some models in Population Dynamics: Stability, Limit cycles and Chaos

  • Jan 2011 - April 2011
  • With: Prof. Gaurav Raina, Arun Chaganty
  • We investigated the behavior of two simple population models - the delayed logistic equation and the Parez-Malta-Coutinho (PMC) model (delay differential equations).
  • We showed that these two systems capture a range of dynamics. The delayed logistic model exhibits fixed points and limit cycles whereas the PMC model also exhibits chaos.
  • We also analytically characterized the same with Hopf bifurcation theory, and investigated the qualitative differences between the two models and resulting implications to control theory.

A study of Analytical Approximations for the Performance Analysis of IEEE 802.11 Wireless Local Area Networks

  • Summer Research Project: May - July 2010
  • With: Prof. Anurag Kumar
  • Application of Mean Field Limit Based Approximations to wireless networks
    • We studied the behavior of a single cell wireless local area network (WLAN) using the \emph{Scaled Node Attempt Model} and characterized the local stability of the equilibrium point.
    • We also performed extensive simulations in order understand how the behavior of a real system (IEEE 802.11e WLAN standard) compares with that of a scaled node attempt model.
  • Analysis of IEEE 802.11n Infrastructure Wireless Local Area Netowrs with Channel Errors
    • We analyzed the performance of 802.11n Infrastructure WLAN with channel errors.
    • We showed that the sufficient conditon for the existence of a unique fixed point for an errorless system holds good even when the system has channel errors.

Applications of Generalized Discrete Fourier Transforms in OFDM

  • Undergraduate Thesis: Aug 2010 - April 2011
  • With: Prof. David Koilpillai
  • We explored the applications of Generalized Discrete Fourier Transforms (GDFT) and evaluated its advantages in Code Division Multiple Access (CDMA) systems.
